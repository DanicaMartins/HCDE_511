4. Where Weaker Hiring Meets Higher Automation Exposure

Which sectors combine weaker hiring and higher automation exposure? Each point is a sector today — position shows patterns worth watching, not proof that AI caused change.

What this tells us

The U.S. market is close to its 2020 baseline.

The national index returned near 100 after the 2021–2022 hiring boom — but that average hides sector-level swings.

Some sectors boomed, then cooled sharply.

Many sectors peaked in 2021–2022 and remain far below their own highs, even when the national total looks calm.

AI adds context, not certainty.

Model release timing and automation exposure help us see patterns worth watching — not proof of cause.

How we measure (index & data)

Posting index: % of Feb 1, 2020 posting volume (100 = same day). Not raw job counts, employment, or wages.

Drawdown: Latest index minus that sector’s peak month index.

Automation score: O*NET degree of automation matched via sector job titles.

AI releases: OpenAI/Anthropic publication months — context only, not causation.

What this does not show
  • Indeed does not publish raw posting counts here — only indexed change from Feb 2020.
  • 100 is a reference day, not a “healthy” labor market target.
  • Correlation between AI releases and posting trends is not causation.
  • National totals near 100 can hide sectors still far below their peaks.
